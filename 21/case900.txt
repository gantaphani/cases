corey wiggin was convicted,after a jury trial,of conspiracy to distribute cocaine.the conspiracy involved at least twenty kilograms of cocaine per year and continued for several years.wiggin,years old at the time of sentencing,received the statutory minimum term of imprisonment of ten years.
on appeal,wiggin presents one challenge to his conviction and one to his sentence.he first argues that the district court,in denying his claim of incompetency to stand trial,misapplied the legal standard by not properly considering whether he had the ability to assist his counsel.the main argument for incompetency was that wiggin suffered mental deficiencies from an accident which led him,against his,to refuse to enter a plea agreement under which he might have received only a sentence.as to his sentence,wiggin urges that ambiguities in the jury instructions and verdict slip could have misled the jury into believing the amount of cocaine involved in the overall conspiracy and the amount attributable to him had to be the same,and that therefore the jury did not truly make a drug quantity finding.we affirm wiggin conviction and sentence.

b.the competency issue 
on september,which was a week after the jury returned its guilty verdict but prior to sentencing,wiggin attorney moved pursuant to for a hearing to determine whether wiggin had been competent to stand trial.the attorney and wiggin mother submitted affidavits stating,inter alia,that wiggin had broken his neck in a motorcycle accident and had subsequently suffered memory loss and a reduction in cognitive capacities,had abused marijuana and cocaine for years,had unwisely rejected the favorable plea offer,had had difficulty remembering events and providing answers to his attorney questions,and had not given his attorney important details about the criminal case prior to trial.wiggin attorney attempted to explain the belated timing of his motion in an affidavit,stating that he was first apprised of wiggin memory loss and his reduced cognitive ability on the fourth day of the trial by his mother and his girl friend.
the district court held an initial hearing on the competency motion on june.two psychologists testified for the defense,having examined wiggin after the trial had concluded.the first,alexandria weida,a forensic psychologist for the commonwealth of massachusetts,stated that she had met with wiggin on two occasions and administered various psychological tests,including the test and the wechsler memory scales.she testified that when she asked wiggin whether he understood terms such as conspiracy,he answered in the affirmative but could not provide a coherent explanation when pressed for details.she also said wiggin was confused about the role of his attorney and the consequences of plea bargaining.weida concluded that at the time of the trial,wiggin was not able rationally to understand the proceedings and charges against him and was not competent to fully assist his counsel.
the second psychologist,frederick kelso,met with wiggin in may and also concluded that wiggin had been incompetent to stand trial.kelso cited two causes a mental defect and cognitive deficits caused by mild traumatic brain injury,and the mental disease of cocaine abuse.kelso testified that wiggin had a minimal understanding of the concept of conspiracy.he stated that he had administered a test that showed wiggin had trouble thinking in abstractions the sort of results he observed were very commonly seen in certain kinds of patients who have suffered a traumatic brain injury.kelso opined that wiggin decision to trust defelice assurances that wiggin did have anything to worry about,even after his attorney told him defelice would testify for the prosecution,was connected to wiggin difficulty in thinking in abstractions.kelso concluded that wiggin assessment of the possibility of accepting a plea deal had not been rational.
william ryan,a forensic psychologist with the federal bureau of prisons,testified for the government.ryan testified that he had interviewed wiggin in the fall of and had subjected him to a test called the minnesota multiphasic personality inventory,edition.he stated that wiggin results indicated that he had basic comprehension skills and could understand and consistently answer questions and he was exaggerating any mental illness he suffered,suggesting either a cry for help or some deliberate attempt to portray himself as very mentally ill.ryan testified that he had spoken with staff members at the prison where wiggin was incarcerated and had been told that wiggin functioned adequately and understood directions.he also testified that,during an interview,wiggin was able to count backward by threes,indicating an ability to concentrate,and was able to give adequate definitions of legal terms such as perjury,witness,verdict,and sentence.wiggin was unable adequately to define several other legal terms when first asked,but when ryan explained them to him and then asked later if he remembered what they meant,wiggin was able to recall the definitions.
according to ryan,wiggin said during the interview that he had not trusted his attorney at trial but later realized his attorney had been trying to help him.asked on whether he found wiggin decision to trust his friends and family instead of his attorney irrational,ryan responded it may not have been a good decision,but there was no irrational,you know,mental illness entering into that decision.based on,inter alia,the testing he administered,his interview of wiggin,and his interviews of prison staff,ryan opined that wiggin suffered from depressive symptoms,stress disorder,and a cognitive disorder possibly due to head trauma.however,he concluded that wiggin was nonetheless functional,rational,and competent.he testified that wiggin conversed,he discussed,he remembered,he retained what he learned,it all impressed me as a person who was competent to stand trial.

a.the competency determination 
the determination of a criminal defendant competency to stand trial is governed by the standards laid out at this provision states that at any time prior to sentencing,the defendant or the attorney for the government may file a motion for a hearing to determine the mental competency of the defendant.the court shall grant the motion,or shall order such a hearing on its own motion,if there is reasonable cause to believe that the defendant is incompetent.id.a.it further provides 
if,after the hearing,the court finds by a preponderance of the evidence that the defendant is presently suffering from a mental disease or defect rendering him mentally incompetent to the extent that he is unable to understand the nature and consequences of the proceedings against him or to assist properly in his defense,the court shall commit the defendant to the custody of the attorney general for hospitalization.
we uphold a district judge determination of competency after a hearing unless that determination is clearly erroneous.united states santos,cir.wiggin,as we understand his argument,contends that the district court erred in concluding that he met the second prong of the competency he was able to assist properly in his defense.he argues that the error is demonstrated by two things that the court referred to the understand the nature and consequences of the proceedings prong of the test a few times without also referring at the same time to the second prong,and that the evidence showed that wiggin could not in fact assist his counsel.he also argues that point demonstrates that the district court incorrectly applied the law to the facts,and that as a result our review in this instance should be plenary,instead of for clear error.
none of these arguments has any merit.as to point and the standard of review issue,the district court at the time it made its competency determination specifically and correctly described both prongs and issued a finding as to each it stated that wiggin simply did not suffer from a mental disease or defect that renders him unable to understand the nature and consequences of the proceeding against him or to assist properly in his defense.further,the court also described the second prong correctly at least three other times during the hearing it noted during separate colloquies with defense counsel and an expert witness that competency requires the capacity to adequately participate in one defense and to meaningfully assist in one defense it also concluded the competency hearing by reiterating that it had found that wiggin did not have a mental disease or defect that deprived him of the ability to adequately assist in his defense.
