in crawford washington,d,the supreme court barred the admission of testimonial hearsay in a criminal case under circumstances in which the accused has not had an opportunity to the declarant.this ruling effected a sea change in the jurisprudence of the confrontation the court left open the parameters of testimonial hearsay,and so its ruling produced a miasma of uncertainty.we enter this murky milieu to answer a question of first impression in this circuit under what circumstances should an excited utterance made to a police officer in this case,a operator be considered testimonial although the question is close,we conclude that,in the circumstances at hand,the excited utterance was nontestimonial and,therefore,properly admitted into evidence.we also reject the defendant other challenges to his conviction and sentence.consequently,we affirm the judgment below.

ii.analysis 
we divide our analysis into three parts,corresponding with the appellant three assignments of error.
a.the anonymous tape.
the appellant primary contention is that the redacted tape of the second call should not have been admitted into evidence as he was not able to confront and the anonymous speaker.this contention derives from the sixth amendment,which provides in pertinent part that i n all criminal prosecutions,the accused shall enjoy the right to be confronted with the witnesses against him.const.amend.vi.in crawford,decided approximately one month after the jury verdict in this case,the supreme court fundamentally altered the jurisprudence of the sixth amendment and took a new approach to determining whether the admission,in a criminal case,of an statement by an unavailable declarant violates an accused right of confrontation.see horton allen,cir.
pertinently,the crawford court decreed that,as to testimonial statements,the confrontation clause assures a procedural right to confrontation rather than a substantive guarantee of evidentiary reliability.see crawford,at.holding,with respect to such statements,that the confrontation clause demands not that evidence be reliable,but that reliability be assessed in a particular manner by testing in the crucible of.as to such statements,the crawford court derided the rule of roberts,which it characterized as d ispensing with confrontation because testimony is obviously reliable,as being akin to dispensing with jury trial because a defendant is obviously guilty.id.at.
the court limited its critique of roberts and its freshly minted procedural right to testimonial statements,basing this limitation upon an examination of both the historical background and the text of the confrontation clause.see id.at.it offered no precise definition of which statements were to be regarded as testimonial and which were not.instead,it observed that the answer to that question would depend heavily on the nature and context of a given statement.see id.at.noting that an accuser who makes a formal statement to government officers bears testimony in a sense that a person who makes a casual remark to an acquaintance does not.
the court then enumerated,for illustrative purposes,a trio of formulations that came within the core class of testimonial statements.id.at.we rehearse these illustrations.
the first formulation encompasses ex parte testimony or its functional is,material such as affidavits,custodial examinations,prior testimony that the defendant was unable to,or similar pretrial statements that declarants would reasonably expect to be used prosecutorially.id.at.the second encompasses extrajudicial statements contained in formalized testimonial materials,such as affidavits,depositions,prior testimony,or confessions.id.at.citation and internal quotation marks omitted alteration in original.the third encompasses statements that were made under circumstances which would lead an objective witness reasonably to believe that the statement would be available for use at a later trial.id.at.citation and internal quotation marks omitted.in the court view,these three formulations all shared a common conceptual nucleus and mapped out the coverage of the confrontation clause at differing levels of abstraction around that nucleus.see id.
the court went on to note that these formulations overlapped and that some statements might fall within any plausible definition of testimonial.id.a statement made during grand jury testimony is an example of this phenomenon.id.so,too,statements given in response to police interrogation would qualify under any of the court stated formulations.id.in this regard,the court cautioned that it used the term interrogation in its colloquial sense,but hastened to add that a statement knowingly given in response to structured police questioning would qualify under any conceivable definition of interrogation.id.at.
against this backdrop,the appellant asseverates that the entirety of the statements made during the anonymous call should be deemed testimonial and,thus,inadmissible because the caller knowingly contacted the police to furnish information about a crime he invokes the third crawford formulation and posits that an objectively reasonable caller would have understood that her statements would be available for use at a later trial.his fallback position is that,at the very least,the statements following the operator initial question should be considered testimonial because that portion of the colloquy was the product of police interrogation.we review de novo the question of whether or not a given statement,in context,should be deemed testimonial.see united states tse,cir see also united states,cir.
the supreme court illustrative formulations suggest that a call that does nothing more than impart a factual account of past criminal activity would constitute testimonial hearsay.after all,the police record and preserve incoming calls for,among other things,potential prosecutorial use.most people not only understand this fact but also understand that because is a pathway to contacting the police in emergency situations,such communications are to be taken seriously.ann.criminalizing the purposeful reporting of false information to a dispatcher.emphasizing these facts,many courts have classified calls of this nature as functionally equivalent to formal statements to the police.see,united states arnold,cir people cortes,misc d,see also richard friedman bridget mccormack.
at first blush,it may seem that the anonymous call at issue here fits this mold.we think not.although the call provided a detailed report of criminal activity including a description of the suspect and information as to his whereabouts,there is more to the story.the district court supportably found that the call qualified as an excited utterance.in this instance,that fact makes a dispositive difference.
the case law in this nascent field is muddled as to whether excited utterances may or may not be classified as testimonial hearsay.the decisions fall into three camps.we briefly describe each such line of authority.
some courts take the view that excited utterances never can constitute testimonial hearsay.their rationale is that,by definition,an excited utterance is made under the influence of a startling event and,thus,the declarant acts in response to that event rather than in response to interrogation or in anticipation of bearing witness.see,united states brun,cir holding that excited utterances are not testimonial in nature because they are emotional and spontaneous rather than deliberate and calculated people moscat,misc d,holding that an excited utterance is a cry for help and,therefore,not functionally equivalent to a formal pretrial examination.in each of these cases,the court upheld the admission of an excited call in a criminal trial notwithstanding the declarant unavailability.see brun,at moscat,at.
a second cluster of cases holds that the excited nature of the utterance has no bearing on whether a particular statement is testimonial.these courts effectively discount the excited nature of the utterance and focus instead on the declarant objectively reasonable expectations.see,arnold,at holding that,notwithstanding the excited nature of a call,the declarant reasonably could expect that statements describing the crime and the perpetrator would be used prosecutorially cortes,at holding that all c alls to to report a crime are testimonial,even if they also qualify as excited utterances,because the purpose of the information is for investigation,prosecution,and potential use at a judicial proceeding.in each of these cases,the court ruled inadmissible in a criminal trial an excited call due to the declarant unavailability.see arnold,at cortes,at.
a third cadre of courts recognizes that the excited utterance inquiry and the testimonial hearsay inquiry are distinct but symbiotic the startling event that gives rise to an excited utterance informs the confrontation clause analysis and often dissipates the very qualities of a statement that otherwise might render the statement testimonial.see,arnold,at sutton,dissenting reasoning that a frightened caller rarely will have the state of mind needed to make a solemn declaration or a statement that he or she would reasonably expect to be used prosecutorially state wright,finding call nontestimonial when declarant was trembling,stuttering,crying and hyperventilating during the call internal quotation marks omitted alteration in original state hembertt,holding excited utterances made to officers during an ongoing threat to be nontestimonial because those utterances were not made in anticipation of eventual prosecution but to assist in securing the scene and apprehending the suspect drayton united states,similar.this approach suggests that courts must undertake a examination of the totality of the circumstances in order to determine whether or not a particular excited utterance should be deemed testimonial in nature.
we reject both per se one that automatically exempts all excited utterances from classification as testimonial hearsay and the one that effectively disregards the excited nature of an utterance.rule allows the admission of excited utterances based on the theory that a person speaking about a startling event,while still under the stress of experiencing or observing that event,normally does not have either the capacity or the incentive to prevaricate.see,united states taveras,cir.it makes very little sense,then,to impute a calm and reasoning state of mind to every such declarant.it does not necessarily follow,however,that just because a statement falls within the literal definition of an excited utterance,the declarant must have lacked the ability to recognize that the statement could be used for prosecutorial purposes.see,lopez state,so d,acknowledging that a declarant excited state has a bearing on his expectation regarding the potential use of his statement in court but nonetheless finding the excited utterance in question testimonial.a wide variety of situations can give rise to excited utterances.if,say,the utterance is removed in time from the startling event,it might qualify as excited,see,united states scarpa,cir,but still might be considered testimonial.crawford,at.implying that a statement of a child victim elicited during police questioning was testimonial despite the fact that it was a spontaneous declaration.
we therefore reject the categorical approaches that lie at either end of the spectrum.instead,we conclude that the excited utterance and testimonial hearsay inquiries are separate,but related.while both inquiries look to the surrounding circumstances to make determinations about the declarant mindset at the time of the statement,their focal points are different.the excited utterance inquiry focuses on whether the declarant was under the stress of a startling event.the testimonial hearsay inquiry focuses on whether a reasonable declarant,similarly situated that is,excited by the stress of a startling event,would have had the capacity to appreciate the legal ramifications of her statement.
these parallel inquiries require an ad hoc,approach.an inquiring court first should determine whether a particular hearsay statement qualifies as an excited utterance.if not,the inquiry ends.if,however,the statement so qualifies,the court then must look to the attendant circumstances and assess the likelihood that a reasonable person would have either retained or regained the capacity to make a testimonial statement at the time of the utterance 
we offer some general guidance for the proper application of this rule.ordinarily,statements made to police while the declarant or others are still in personal danger can not be said to have been made with consideration of their legal ramifications.such a declarant usually speaks out of urgency and a desire to obtain a prompt response.it follows,therefore,that such statements will not normally be deemed testimonial.see hembertt,at.once the immediate danger has subsided,however,a person who speaks while still under the stress of a startling event is more likely able to comprehend the larger significance of her words.if the record fairly supports a finding of comprehension,the fact that the statement also qualifies as an excited utterance will not alter its testimonial nature.see drayton,at.
as a final matter,we caution against the use of an all or nothing approach to the admission or exclusion of calls.it is entirely possible that some portions of a call may qualify as excited utterances,while others do not.similarly,some portions may be deemed testimonial,while others may be deemed nontestimonial.this means,of course,that some parts of a single call may run headlong into the crawford bar,while others do not.
our dictum in united states luciano,cir,is in alignment with this framework.there,a teenager witnessed a man pointing a gun at a woman,flagged down a passing police cruiser,and reported what he had seen.id.at.in dictum,we observed that the statement was an excited utterance,made to secure immediate assistance for a woman in danger and,thus,was not testimonial id.at.
this discussion adumbrates the result in the case before us.the anonymous caller stated that she had just heard gunshots and seen a man with a gun,that the man had pointed the gun at her,and that the man was still in her line of sight.her account strongly suggests that she and her son,as well as others in the vicinity,were in imminent personal peril when the call was made.the immediacy of the threat,the existence of a clear and present danger,and the fact that no substantial time had elapsed,in combination,severely erode any basis for a finding that the declarant was in a calm and reasoning state when she placed the call.and,furthermore,the precision of the declarant physical description of the suspect is more consistent with a sense of urgency and a desire to obtain a prompt removal of a dangerous man from the with an anticipation that her call might be used prosecutorially.
these factors are sufficient to ground our decision.based on the foregoing,we conclude that,here,the circumstances that made the anonymous call an excited utterance were significant enough to overwhelm the caller capacity to appreciate the potential use of her words.it follows that the call was nontestimonial and,therefore,properly admissible as an excited utterance.see crawford,at.making clear that nontestimonial statements falling within firmly rooted hearsay exceptions remain admissible.
the appellant fallback position is equally unavailing.it can not fairly be said in the circumstances of this case that any part of the call was the product of structured police interrogation.questions by a operator that merely clarify or focus an excited caller statement are not interrogation in any meaningful sense of the word.see,people corella,th,d.here,it would blink reality to place under the rubric of interrogation the single question asked by the question that only momentarily interrupted an otherwise continuous stream of consciousness.
that ends this aspect of the matter.because the admitted portion of the anonymous call was both an excited utterance and nontestimonial in nature,the call introduction into evidence did not sully the appellant sixth amendment right to confrontation,notwithstanding the declarant unavailability for.
b.the prior convictions.
the appellant also challenges the district court admission of evidence of his three prior convictions.the applicable rule provides that if a criminal defendant elects to testify,evidence that he has been convicted of a crime punishable by more than one year in prison shall be admitted if the court determines that the probative value of admitting this evidence outweighs its prejudicial effect to the accused.a.in are exceptions,but none is relevant evidence is only admissible if the convictions in question are less than ten years old.see.b.
by its terms,rule a demands that the trial court construct a balance.we review a trial court construction of this balance,its ruling admitting or excluding rule evidence for abuse of discretion.see united states powell,cir.
the three prior convictions here at issue were all felony convictions.though somewhat remote in time from the date of the instant three convictions occurred in were within the window framed by rule b.the question,then,is whether the district court abused its discretion in determining that the probative value of those convictions for impeachment purposes outweighed their prejudicial effects.
the appellant suggests that our decision in united states tavares,cir en banc,bears on this question.in tavares,we held that once a defendant who is facing a charge has stipulated to the fact of a preexisting felony conviction,the government may not introduce evidence as to the nature of that conviction for the purpose of establishing the felon element of the charge.id.at.even when the defendant has so stipulated,however,the government still may introduce evidence of prior felony convictions for impeachment purposes if the defendant elects to testify.see united states tracy,cir.
in this instance,the appellant stipulated that he previously had been found guilty of a felony.he subsequently chose to testify in his own defense.the government then sought to discredit that testimony by introducing the three prior convictions for impeachment purposes.given this scenario,tavares is of little utility.
we turn now to the disputed ruling.although the appellant questions how past crimes that do not directly involve dishonesty or fraud are relevant to credibility,our case law long has recognized that rule represents a valid legislative judgment that such convictions do have some probative value for impeachment purposes.see united states norton,cir united states oakes,cir.the nature of the underlying felony generally goes not to its admissibility per se but,rather,to its weight in the balancing of probative worth and prejudicial impact.see jack weinstein margaret berger,weinstein federal evidence b ed collecting cases.
a wide array of factors may be considered when calibrating the rule scales.without limiting the generality of that statement,these may include i the impeachment value of the particular convictions ii their immediacy or remoteness even though they are within the window iii the degree of potential prejudice that they portend iv the importance of the defendant testimony and v the salience of the credibility issue in the circumstances of the particular case.see,united states smith,cir united states jackson.
in this case,several of these factors cut in favor of admissibility.prior crimes are generally viewed as having some bearing on veracity.see,united states gant,cir finding offense probative of credibility in gun possession case united states lattner,cir similar.the offenses underlying the appellant prior convictions are not similar to the offenses charged in this case.that is relevant because convictions for dissimilar crimes are customarily thought to be less prejudicial than convictions for similar crimes which may run a risk of implying a propensity to commit the crime.see,united states montgomery,cir jackson,at.perhaps most important,this case hinged on a credibility choice the jury had to decide whether to believe the appellant or the police officers.the salience of the credibility issue weighs in favor of admitting the prior convictions.see united states pritchard,cir oakes,at.
to be sure,other factors,remoteness weigh against admissibility.taking the panoply of circumstances as a whole,however,the decision to accept or reject the proffer fell well within the encincture of the district court discretion.where the circumstances can fairly support a decision either to admit or to exclude particular evidence,it is not the proper province of an appellate court to the trial court judgment.freeman package mach,cir observing,with respect to rule balancing,that o nly in extraordinarily compelling we,from the vista of a cold appellate record,reverse a district court judgment concerning the relative weighing of probative value and unfair effect.so it is here 
the appellant makes two subsidiary arguments that warrant brief comment.first,he assigns error to the district court failure to spell out its exposition of the rule a balancing test.although it would have been helpful for the court to make a detailed balancing analysis on the record,the court was not required to do so.see united states de la cruz,cir.moreover,the absence of express findings is less troublesome where,as here,the record reflects the court keen awareness of its duty to balance probative value against prejudicial effect and its discussion of the issue with counsel.
second,the appellant,citing powell,argues that the lower court erred in allowing the government to describe the nature of the prior felony convictions by using the title of each crime.in powell,we observed that the practice of allowing the government to ask about the number of felonies,but not to describe their nature,was a fair way to help balance probative value against prejudicial impact.at.although powell endorses the admission of rule a evidence in this truncated manner,it in no way requires the use of such a technique.here,moreover,the appellant attempt to raise this argument stands on noticeably shaky ground the district court expressly offered him the chance to have only the number of felonies recounted,but he eschewed the opportunity.
sentencing.
finally,the appellant suggests that the district court erred in sentencing him pursuant to the mandatory sentencing guidelines then in effect.see united states booker,d.he concedes that he did not preserve this claim of error below thus,it engenders plain error review.see united states martins,cir united states antonakopoulos,cir.in order to prevail under that standard,the appellant must make a showing that an error occurred which was clear or obvious and which not only affected the defendant substantial rights,but also seriously impaired the fairness,integrity,or public reputation of judicial proceedings.united states duarte,cir.
with respect to a claim of booker error,the first two elements of the plain error test are satisfied whenever a defendant has been sentenced pursuant to a mandatory guidelines system.antonakopoulos,at.that is the case here.the appellant stumbles,however,on the third step of the plain error staircase.
to demonstrate that a booker error affected substantial rights,a defendant must show a reasonable probability that he would have received a more lenient sentence under an advisory guidelines regime.united states,cir antonakopoulos,at.in this type of situation,we are not overly demanding as to proof of probability.united states heldeman,cir.still,a defendant must offer something that has persuasive force.see id.requiring,at a bare minimum,a reasonable indication that the district judge might well have reached a different result under advisory guidelines.
the appellant argues that there is a reasonable probability of a more lenient sentence because the district court i sentenced him at the nadir of the applicable sentencing range and ii encountered severe limitations,under the guidelines,as to its ability to consider his personal characteristics and disadvantaged upbringing.these arguments lack force the appellant generic challenges simply can not cross the antonakopoulos threshold.
we need not tarry.we have held,with a regularity bordering on the monotonous,that a defendant can not establish a reasonable probability of a more lenient sentence merely by showing that the district court sentenced him at the low end of the guideline sentencing range.see,united states guzm√°n,cir united states kornegay,cir.we also have held that when a sentencing court gives no indication that it is impressed by a defendant disadvantageous upbringing,fully detailed in the presentence investigation report,we can not draw an inference from the court silence that it would have imposed a more lenient sentence under an advisory guidelines system.see martins,at.on this record,then,the appellant has failed to carry his burden of showing a reasonable probability that he would have received a lower sentence under an advisory guidelines regime.
iii.conclusion 
we need go no further.this is a close and difficult case,but the appellant,though ably represented,has not convinced us that any reversible error inhered in either the admission of evidence or the imposition of sentence.
i agree that the statements by the anonymous caller reporting brito offense were and therefore were admitted consistent with crawford washington,d.see ante at.more specifically,i agree that the statements here were properly admitted even if the nature of the inquiry is that,as the lead opinion says,the testimonial hearsay inquiry focuses on whether a reasonable declarant,similarly situated,would have had the capacity to appreciate the legal ramifications of her statement.ante at.i write separately,however,because i do not read crawford to make the declarant actual or hypothetically reasonable state of mind the loadstar by which we are to determine if a statement is testimonial.compare id.at.rather,as i read crawford,the focus should be on the circumstances in which the statement was obtained,with the declarant state of mind at least in cases where that state of mind is shown to be no more than an awareness,rather than an intention being only one factor in determining the nature of the circumstances.see crawford,at.defining a testimonial statement by reference to the nature of the proceeding in which the statement was made.
in crawford,the supreme court revised its approach to deciding when a hearsay statement may be admitted at a criminal trial without violating the confrontation clause guarantee that the accused shall enjoy the right to be confronted with the witnesses against him.const.amend.vi.prior to crawford,the court permitted the admission of hearsay from an unavailable declarant so long as it fell within a firmly rooted hearsay exception or otherwise bore particularized guarantees of the trustworthiness.ohio roberts,d.crawford overruled roberts as to testimonial hearsay,holding that such hearsay is not to be admitted unless the declarant is unavailable for trial and the defendant had a prior opportunity to.see at.the court did not,however,set forth a definitive definition of the term testimonial.see id.at.instead,it provided three formulations of the core class of testimonial statements at various levels of abstraction 
ex parte testimony or its is material such as affidavits,custodial examinations,prior testimony that the defendant was unable to,or similar pretrial statements that declarants would reasonably expect to be used prosecutorially extrajudicial statements contained in formalized testimonial materials,such as affidavits,depositions,prior testimony,or confessions and statements that were made under circumstances which would lead an objective witness reasonably to believe that the statement would be available for use at a later trial.
as the lead opinion observes,these formulations have engendered a miasma of uncertainty among lower courts trying to identify testimonial hearsay.ante at see also crawford,at.recognizing that the court failure to articulate a comprehensive definition of testimonial statements will cause interim uncertainty.many courts have resolved this uncertainty by seizing on the most general statement is testimonial if the circumstance under which the statement was made would lead an objective witness reasonably to believe that the statement would be available for use at a later applying it,without sufficient attention to crawford textual and historical rationale.see,united states summers,cir united states cromer,cir united states saget,cir commonwealth gonsalves.courts that have adopted this approach have divided on close questions such as the categorization of various calls.ante at.a sounder,more predictable body of law will emerge if,when applying the various formulations,we hew closely to what i suggest is the foundational thrust of crawford.
crawford employed a historical approach to define the reach of the confrontation clause.see id.at.in reviewing the historical record,the court identified the mode of criminal procedure as the principal evil at which the confrontation clause was directed.id.at.in particular,the elimination of ex parte examinations was the clause main concern.id.
the court discussed typical examples of the abusive use of interrogations in early english law.id.at.it emphasized the practice of justices of the peace examining suspects and witnesses before trial and then reading these examinations in court.id.at.the court highlighted the notorious trial of sir walter raleigh,in which raleigh was convicted of treason based on a hearsay statement given by lord cobham to the privy council.id.at.dissatisfaction with these practices spawned the common law rule,in practice when the sixth amendment was adopted in,that an statement given to a government official as part of a formal interrogation could only be introduced if the defendant had previously been able to the declarant.id.at.
the historical examples in crawford all involved the government use of formal ex parte examinations to obtain hearsay statements that could later be used at a criminal trial.according to the court,this historical background demonstrates that the confrontation clause was intended to prohibit the government use of these procedures where the defendant did not have an opportunity for.see id.at.
to support its historical interpretation,the court examined the confrontation clause text.id.at.the clause applies to witnesses,which the court defined as one who bears testimony.id.in turn,the court defined testimony as a solemn declaration or affirmation made for the purpose of establishing or proving a fact.id.the court then synthesized the clause text and history to reach the general conclusion that the clause reflects an especially acute concern with a of statement,solemn declarations made to government officials in circumstances that resemble the repudiated mode of interrogation.id.
it is only after this discussion that the court set forth its three formulations of testimonial hearsay.id.at.the first two are examples of practices that resemble the mode of interrogation,affidavits,depositions,custodial examinations,prior testimony,confessions,and police interrogations.id.only the third presents a general definition without concrete examples.id.in the historical and textual discussion preceding these formulations,i find no warrant to interpret this third formulation to substantially broaden crawford holding.instead,we should construe the three formulations as directed at the same concern preventing the government from using hearsay statements at trial if the statements were obtained through a method that resembles a mode of interrogation see gonsalves,at,sosman,concurring stating that courts should strive to harmonize all three crawford formulations instead of allowing the third to swallow the other two see also united states saner,d,stating that a statement is testimonial if it was made in circumstances similar to the abuses at which the confrontation clause was directed people kilday,d,holding that statement obtained by a police officer was because the officer was not acting in an investigative capacity when the statement was made.under this view,the third formulation ordinarily should be limned by whether the statement was obtained by means of a procedure that resembles formal ex parte interrogation.
