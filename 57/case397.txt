our review of the district court grant of a motion to dismiss is de novo.fitzgerald harris,cir.to survive a motion to dismiss,the complaint must allege a plausible entitlement to relief.id.quoting bell atl.twombly,d.we turn first to the adea claim and then briefly discuss the due process claim.our disposition of these claims makes it unnecessary to consider appellants arguments concerning available remedies.
on appeal,plaintiffs offer numerous theories to support their contention that the defendants violated their rights under the adea.among these claims,we discern three rationales for recovery that warrant our attention plaintiffs terminations in accordance with law were unlawful because the adea bars a state or local government from lowering a retirement age that was in effect as of march,plaintiffs mandatory retirement violated the adea because they were not provided with fitness testing to determine their capacity to continue working,and plaintiffs terminations did not comply with j of the adea because their discharges were not pursuant to a bona fide hiring or retirement plan that is not a subterfuge to evade the purposes of this chapter.
a.the lowered retirement age of law and the adea 
plaintiffs maintain that the and amendments to the adea,which created the exemption for termination of public safety personnel,prohibit the reduction of a mandatory retirement age that was applicable on march.they contend that j permits enactment of new age limits only in states and localities that had no mandatory retirement law in effect at that time.because puerto rico mandatory retirement age for police officers was in march,plaintiffs assert that law,with its lowered age limit,must be invalidated as inconsistent with the federal law 
in construing a statute,we begin with its plain meaning,and i f the meaning of the text is unambiguous our task ends there as well.united states godin,cir.a statute is not ambiguous unless it admits of more than one reasonable interpretation.id.quoting motors darling,cir.the language of j,as amended in,does not support plaintiffs proposed interpretation of the provision.the statute explicitly provides that states or their subdivisions may discharge a law enforcement officer or firefighter pursuant to a mandatory retirement plan that either was in effect on march,or was enacted after september.the only limitation on the latter option is that the discharge occur no earlier than age.j b ii.nothing in the language of the provision even suggests that governments that had mandatory retirement laws in place as of march,could not enact new laws with lower retirement ages after september.

b.the fitness testing requirement 
plaintiffs contend that,even if they were lawfully subject to retirement at age,the commonwealth had to give them the opportunity to avoid discharge by taking performance tests that could prove their physical and mental fitness to continue working.this is the testing described above,which congress expected to be in place within about four years after enactment of the amendment.although plaintiffs acknowledge that the particular tests referenced in the statute have never been identified by the secretary of hhs,they insist that testing is nonetheless a prerequisite to their discharge.they claim that,in the absence of federally developed tests,puerto rico was required to devise its own evaluation refrain from imposing mandatory retirement.
we reject this reading of the statute.although the language of j requires careful parsing,it unambiguously requires testing as a to mandatory retirement only for those employees who would be discharged after the secretary of hhs promulgates appropriate tests as described above,section j states that the testing requirement applies if the individual was discharged after the date described in such section,and such section is section d of the age discrimination in employment amendments of.j the date referenced in section d is the date of issuance of the regulations the secretary shall issue identifying valid,nondiscriminatory job performance tests that shall be used by employers seeking the exemption,stat.thus,when pieced together,the elements of the statutory requirement are that employers seeking the benefit of the provision must use the tests identified by the secretary once regulations identifying those tests have been issued.
although the statutory language is not in our view reasonably susceptible to another interpretation,our conclusion would be the same if we deemed the provision ambiguous and found it necessary to consider legislative history to ascertain congress intent.see godin,at noting that,i f the statute is ambiguous,we look beyond the text to the legislative history in order to determine congressional intent see also minch,at stating that t he failure to promulgate guidelines and regulations for fitness testing gives rise to an ambiguity in the statute drnek chicago,d,concluding that the plain language of is ambiguous because it leaves a gap by requiring employers to comply with regulations that have not been promulgated.
in a thoughtful discussion that was endorsed by the seventh circuit in minch,see at,the district court in drnek examined j legislative history at length before concluding that the public safety exemption in the provision was subject to the use of fitness tests when and if suitable tests were ever made available by hhs.drnek,d at.the court cited comments made during floor debates on the proposed legislation that showed skepticism among members of congress about the adequacy of existing physical tests.see id.at the court then concluded 
t he legislative history suggests that the intent of the amendment was to reinstate an exemption from the adea allowing for retirement for public safety officials because fitness tests were unreliable,expensive,and had potential discriminatory effects on women and minorities.the provision in j for compliance with d merely imposed an obligation on employers to provide tests when and if suitable tests became available it did not make tests a condition precedent to the operation of the exemption.
we thus agree with the seventh circuit that this component of section j is essentially meaningless at this juncture.minch,at.at present,a state or local government seeking to enforce a mandatory retirement provision must show only that the designated retirement age was set forth in a law that either was a in effect on march,or b in a law enacted after september,and is no lower than age and the termination was taken pursuant to a bona fide retirement plan that is not a subterfuge for impermissible age discrimination.having already addressed the timing and scope of law,we now consider appellants contention that the statute was a subterfuge to evade the purposes of the adea 
c.the subterfuge requirement 
plaintiffs argue that the enactment of law was a subterfuge to evade the requirements of the adea because the defendants discriminated in a manner forbidden by the act when they lowered the mandatory retirement age from to.plaintiffs emphasize that defendants acted with discriminatory animus,deliberately seeking to replace them with younger officers,and they assert that this discriminatory motive connotes an impermissible scheme to bypass defendants obligations under the adea.
in effect,plaintiffs allege that law was a subterfuge to evade the adea because it was the product of intentional age discrimination.using age as a basis for requiring retirement is precisely what section j entitles the commonwealth to do,however,and we thus join the second and seventh circuits in rejecting an interpretation of subterfuge that would effectively nullify the exemption.see feldman,at there is nothing deceptive about the state legislature doing exactly what congress provided it the authority to do minch,at d oing something that the statute expressly permits does not evade its prohibitions.
in its analysis of the subterfuge limitation in minch,the seventh circuit relied on the supreme court decision in public employees retirement system betts,d.minch,at see also feldman,at adopting the reasoning in minch.the court in betts had considered another provision of the f that also contained a subterfuge exception to an exemption from liability.minch,at.section f protected employment decisions from the prohibitions of the adea if they were taken pursuant to the terms of a bona fide benefit plan and the same language that appears in section j the plan was not a subterfuge to evade the purposes of the act.id.
in interpreting the not a subterfuge language in section f,the supreme court rejected the notion that differences in employee benefits would be inconsistent with the adea unless the employer could offer a cost justification for the differential treatment.betts,at.the court concluded that,to avoid a interpretation of the exception,id.at,the not a subterfuge criterion could not invalidate the very that the exemption allowed.id.at see also minch,at.the seventh circuit applied that reasoning in the context of section j 
the adea does not forbid a state or locality from making retirement decisions as to its police and firefighting personnel it expressly allows state and local governments to make such decisions so long as they act within the parameters set forth in section j the statute does not condition the validity of such retirement programs on proof that the public employer has adopted the program genuinely believing that it is justified in the interest of public safety.instead,recognizing that there was not yet any national consensus as to the relationship between age and one fitness to serve as a police officer or firefighter,congress opted simply to restore the status quo ante,permitting states and cities to continue imposing age limits on these positions as they had been able to do prior to the adea extension to state and municipal employers and wyoming holding sustaining that extension.
minch,at.thus,proof that an employer imposes mandatory retirement for police officers and firefighters based on the view that older individuals are not up to the rigors of law enforcement or firefighting and should make room for younger,fresher replacements will not establish subterfuge because it does not reveal a kind of discriminatory conduct that the adea by its very terms forbids.id.
as both the supreme court in betts and the seventh circuit in minch emphasized,this understanding does not strip the subterfuge provision of all meaning.instead,a plaintiff asserting subterfuge must show that the employer is using the exemption as a way to evade another substantive provision of the act in other words,that the employer is commit ting some other type of age discrimination forbidden by the adea.minch,at see also betts,at.feldman,at.the seventh circuit offered two examples of viable claims of subterfuge in the context of section j if an employer adopted or reinstated age limits in order to retaliate against an employee who had protested practices made unlawful by the adea or if the employer adopted age limits for police and firefighting personnel at the same time that it created a new,position unrestricted by age,and invited the newly retired officers to apply,allowing the inference that the mandatory retirement scheme was a subterfuge for wage discrimination against older employees.minch,at citing betts,at.
as we have described,the plaintiffs here offer only accusations of animus to support their subterfuge contention,alleging in their complaint that the governor and police superintendent agreed to get rid of a group of old timers with the purpose of replacing them by younger officers.even if that allegation fairly describes the defendants motivation,the fact remains that the adea permits government employers who are concerned about the effectiveness of older public safety officers to impose mandatory retirement at age.plaintiffs have thus offered no theory of subterfuge that can withstand a motion to dismiss,and they have therefore failed to state a viable claim under the adea.
plaintiffs assert that their fourteenth amendment right to due process was violated when,pursuant to law,the defendants lowered the mandatory retirement age by ten years without providing them an opportunity to demonstrate their physical and mental fitness to continue working.they claim that law,the act that set as the upper age limit for police officers,gave them a property interest in their jobs until they reached that age.plaintiffs also assert that they were entitled to individual hearings before their terminations.
we see no merit in this constitutional claim.to the extent plaintiffs have a property interest in their jobs,it derives from commonwealth law.see,cir property interests are created and defined by existing rules or understandings that stem from an independent source such as state law.quoting bd.of regents roth,d.the right to continued employment until age that plaintiffs claim to have been given by law was modified by law,and no due process violation occurs when the legislature which creates a statutory entitlement or other property interest alter s or terminat es the entitlement by subsequent legislative enactment.gattis gravett,cir see also id.at while the legislative alteration or elimination of a previously conferred property interest may be a deprivation,the legislative process itself provides citizens with all of the process they are due.citing atkins parker,d story green,cir having enacted a statute that created such a property right,the legislature retains the power to enact new legislation altering or eliminating the right,and that elimination does not contravene the due process or takings clauses of the constitution.hoffman city of warwick,cir where the legislature enacts general legislation eliminating statutory rights or otherwise adjusting the benefits and burdens of economic life,in the absence of any substantive constitutional infirmity,the legislative determination provides all the process that is due.quoting logan zimmerman brush,d see also passenger atchison,topeka santa fe,d a bsent some clear indication that the legislature intends to bind itself contractually,the presumption is that a law is not intended to create private contractual or vested rights but merely declares a policy to be pursued until the legislature shall ordain otherwise.quoting dodge bd.of.educ.ass island ex rel.scigulinsky retirement bd.of,cir.
moreover,w hen statutory benefits are denied or terminated pursuant to a policy determination,as opposed to an individual determination of eligibility,the due process clause does not require the state to afford a hearing to each affected individual.hoffman,at see also johnson lefkowitz,cir rejecting entitlement to hearing before mandatory retirement because the administrative cost to the state of providing each retiree with a hearing would be enormous,and by far outweigh the hardship to the individual town court nursing,d blackmun,concurring in the judgment when governmental action affects more than a few individuals,concerns beyond economy,efficiency,and expedition tip the balance against finding that due process attaches.thus,the police department failure to administer fitness tests before discharging plaintiffs violates neither the constitution nor,as we have explained,the adea.
plaintiffs also attempt to rest a due process claim on the arbitrariness of age as the point at which police officers are no longer physically fit to serve on the force.they cite the puerto rico legislature subsequent amendment of law,by act of june,raising the upper age limit for police officers and firefighters to,as evidence that the defendants lacked a basis for terminating their employment at age.although this claim is insufficiently developed to warrant our consideration,it is,in any event,patently unavailing.section j explicitly permits states to impose mandatory retirement on public safety personnel at age,and a state legislature is therefore not obliged to amass scientific research or empirical evidence justifying its decision to adopt that age.the judgment made by puerto rico legislature,after further reflection,that the commonwealth safety goals could be better achieved by raising the age to hardly demonstrates that the prior adoption of age was arbitrary.forceful arguments certainly can be made as to the wisdom of enforcing a maximum age of hire,kopec,at,but puerto rico decision to do so consistently with the adea did not violate plaintiffs right to due process.
we summarize our holdings 
puerto rico law,which lowered the mandatory retirement age for public safety personnel from to,is consistent with section j of the adea 
defendants were not required to administer fitness tests before enforcing law mandatory retirement age because the testing requirement in section j will not take effect until appropriate tests are identified in regulations promulgated by the secretary of hhs 
plaintiffs allegations of animus on the part of defendants do not establish that law was a subterfuge for unlawful discrimination within the meaning of section j because the exemption purpose is to allow the termination of police officers and firefighters on the basis on age 
the due process clause of the fourteenth amendment was not violated by plaintiffs terminations without fitness testing or individualized hearings.
we thus conclude that the district court properly dismissed plaintiffs complaint under federal rule of civil procedure b for failure to state a viable claim for relief.the court likewise properly denied plaintiffs motion for reconsideration.
the judgment of the district court is therefore affirmed.
