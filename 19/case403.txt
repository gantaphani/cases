ii.analysis 
a.the plea.standard of review 
because alvira sanchez raised no objection to his plea colloquy below,he bears the burden of showing that an error occurred that the error was clear or obvious that the error impaired his substantial rights and that the error seriously impaired the fairness,integrity,or public reputation of judicial proceedings.united states duarte,cir.
the consequences of count 
before entering a guilty plea,a court must ensure that the defendant understands any maximum possible penalty,including imprisonment,fine,and term of supervised release.b h.here,rather than explaining the consequences of the gun counts counts and separately,the district court said merely,in relevant part,r egarding the gun,the statutory penalty is at least five years,statutory minimum not more than life imprisonment supervised release that can go as high as five years plus the payment of a special monetary assessment that consecutive.the court accurately stated the penalties for possession of a firearm in furtherance of a drug trafficking crime count.see c a i a a b a.the court did not,however,indicate that possession of a firearm in a school zone count carried the potential for a separate term of imprisonment not more than five years,required to run consecutively,see a,and its own concurrent term of supervised release,see a.
the government argues that the court statement that the gun counts may require a consecutive sentence was adequate notice,but alvira sanchez is correct that nothing in that formulation served to inform him that the gun counts carried the potential for separate multiple sentences rather than a single sentence,and that the consecutive nature of those sentences was mandatory as opposed to permissive,and that they must be consecutive not only to all counts,but also to each other.failure to explain correctly the interaction of the consecutive sentences on the two counts was thus obviously erroneous.united states santiago,cir government conceded that failure to inform defendant that sentences must run consecutively was an obvious error.
that error,though,did not affect alvira sanchez substantial rights.given that count does not require any minimum sentence,see a,and that alvira sanchez already knew he risked up to a year term for counts and a consecutive life sentence for count,there is no reason to think that the risk of an additional year term would have affected alvira sanchez willingness to plead.he does not allege as much.see united states romero galindez,cir finding no plain error where district court understated the supervised release period by two years at the change of plea hearing because,inter alia,the discrepancy was but a small fraction of the penalty defendant was facing.
the same analysis holds for the district court failure to describe accurately count term of supervised release.alvira sanchez knew that he faced a potential term of supervised release under count that would never be more than five years.because supervised release terms must run concurrently,see u.e,and because no authorized term of supervised release under exceeds five years,any term of supervised release under count could not have increased alvira sanchez total exposure.alvira sanchez nevertheless argues that the number of concurrent supervised release terms being served is consequential,as it could affect the consequences should he violate his conditions of release.alvira sanchez fails,however,to allege that such an indirect and potential ramification of the plea would have affected his decision to plead guilty,had he been properly informed of count concurrent supervised release term.nor would such an allegation be remotely persuasive.
nor did the failure to inform alvira sanchez of count potential for an added monetary assessment likely affect his decision to plead.the court mentioned a monetary assessment in connection with the gun charges and did not specify any dollar amount.alvira sanchez elected to plead without knowing what his financial liability might be.it is not likely that the omission impaired alvira sanchez substantial rights because it is not likely that he would have reversed course had he known that he faced two assessments of unknown value in connection with the gun charges,rather than one.
the right to persist in pleading not guilty 
alvira sanchez complains for the first time on appeal that the district court never in so many words confirmed that he understood that,as federal rule of criminal procedure b b states in a straightforward manner,he had the right to plead not guilty,or having already so pleaded,to persist in that plea.rather,the district court asked alvira sanchez if he understood that he was waiving the right that you have to put the government through the burden of proving you guilty beyond a reasonable doubt without you having to prove your innocence.whether this description of the right was,by itself,an insufficient circumlocution or,instead,a practical elaboration that might stand on its own,we need not decide rather,the transcript as a whole makes clear that alvira sanchez knew that he was not compelled to persist in pleading guilty.united states cotal crespo,cir finding plea colloquy adequate when it was clear from context,inter alia,that the defendants understood that they had the right to persist in their innocence and go to trial,but cautioning that for the sake of judicial economy and fundamental fairness,the best way to ensure that rule is complied with is to explicitly comply with rule.
notably,the district court asked alvira sanchez toward the conclusion of the colloquy,do you still want to plead thereby implying that it was still alvira sanchez choice whether or not to plead.even alvira sanchez brief on appeal implicitly concedes this point by arguing that the court explanation at the hearing of the criminal charges against him affected his decision to plead guilty.accordingly,even if we were to assume that the lack of an express reference to the right to persist in a plea of not guilty was error,such an error could not have affected alvira sanchez substantial rights.united states borrero acevedo,cir it is defendant burden on plain error review to show that but for the error he would otherwise not have pled guilty.if the record contains no evidence in defendant favor,his claim fails.
b.the monetary assessment and supervised release 
alvira sanchez briefly argues that the supervised release term and monetary assessment sentences imposed for count were contrary to statute.a reads in relevant part,except for the authorization of a term of imprisonment of not more than years made in this paragraph,for the purpose of any other law a violation of section q possession of a firearm in a school zone shall be deemed to be a misdemeanor.thus reading his q count count to be a misdemeanor,alvira sanchez asserts that the maximum permitted supervised release term under b is year,and the maximum permitted monetary assessment under a a iii is.
but the statutory language is not as clear as alvira sanchez contends.a classifies an offense as a class d felony if the maximum term of imprisonment authorized is less than ten years but five or more years.one could construe a as establishing that a q offense is a misdemeanor except where misdemeanor and felony are distinguished by their authorized terms of imprisonment.this may not be the most persuasive reading,but this circuit has never addressed the question.some courts have taken alvira sanchez reading.see united states rivera concepcion,ccc,wl,at but see id.at stating amount of monetary assessment for q violation as.others,however,have at least implicitly understood a q violation to constitute a class d felony for sentencing purposes.see hough united states,cv fdw,wl,at three years supervised release for q violation.the statute imprecise language and the fact that other courts have fallen prey to the same error,if error indeed there was here,demonstrate that any misconstruction on the part of the district court was not obviously erroneous.united states richard,cir declining to resolve a question of statutory interpretation on plain error review because the law in this circuit was not obvious when the district court imposed its sentence.
c.the government factual proffer 
alvira sanchez argues that it was plain error for the district court to accept his guilty plea despite the fact that the government proffered no facts showing that he possessed a firearm outside his residence.see q b prohibition on possessing a firearm in a school zone does not apply on private property not part of school grounds.however,the question relevant to the sufficiency of the evidence in a plea proffer is not whether a jury would,or even would be likely,to convict it is whether there is enough evidence so that the plea has a rational basis in facts.united states gandia maysonet,cir.many crimes are proved by reasonably probative circumstantial evidence,and a rational jury could conclude due to the nature of the seized gun and evidence of alvira sanchez involvement in the drug trade that he did not keep his gun dutifully at home.other juries appear to have inferred q violations based on guns found at private residences.see,united states nieves casta√±o,cir.
even if the district court did commit error by accepting the government proffer,the error was not obvious.a proffer establishes a sufficient factual basis for a guilty plea if it touches all the elements of the crime.see united states piper,cir the district court need not gratuitously explore points removed from the elements of the offense.alvira sanchez points to no circuit precedent establishing whether possession outside the home is an element of a q offense,or whether possession inside the home is an affirmative defense.if the latter,then the government had no obligation to proffer evidence to rebut an anticipated defense.and even if the former,the law is sufficiently unsettled that any error in accepting the government proffer was not obvious.richard,at no obvious error when law in circuit unsettled.
